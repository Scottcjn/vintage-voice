# VintageVoice Cajun French — Evaluation Report (2026-06-17)

First quantitative eval of the Cajun French TTS. 18 held-out Cajun sentences,
4 conditions, two objective metrics + a native-speaker A/B sheet (pending).
Tooling: CosyVoice2 inference (speed 0.95), whisper-medium ASR, `jiwer` WER,
Praat (`parselmouth`) formants + librosa MFCC.

## Conditions
| tag | model | lexicon respell |
|---|---|---|
| `base` | CosyVoice2-0.5B (untuned) | on |
| `ep2` | Cajun finetune (8.1 h), **production** | on |
| `ep2_noresp` | Cajun finetune, lexicon **off** | off |
| `prairie` | prairie-ep1 (8.1 h + 1.03 h St-Landry ×3) | on |

## 1. Intelligibility — WER (ASR back-transcription, vs intended text; lower better)

| condition | WER (vs intended) |
|---|---|
| base (untuned) | **0.600** |
| **ep2 (production)** | **0.202** |
| ep2, lexicon off | 0.183 |
| prairie-ep1 | 0.227 |

**Headline result — the finetune works, measurably.** The Cajun finetune cuts
WER from **0.60 → 0.20** vs the untuned base — a ~66% relative reduction in
word error. On an 8 GB consumer GPU with ~8 h of data. That is a real,
reportable low-resource result.

**Lexicon trades standard-French WER for authenticity.** Lexicon *on* (0.202)
scores slightly *worse* than *off* (0.183) — but this is expected and arguably
*validates* the lexicon: it respells toward Cajun pronunciation (*cher→cha*,
*comment ça va→Comeaux*), which a standard-French ASR transcribes as different
words, inflating WER against Parisian spelling. WER (a Parisian-French metric)
is the wrong tool to credit Cajun authenticity — that needs the human A/B (below).

**Hardest sentences:** place-names (whisper: Opelousas→"Obelusa",
Atchafalaya→"Chafalaya", Lafayette→"Ali al-Afayat") and folklore terms — both
genuinely hard and partly ASR failure, not synthesis failure.

## 2. Accent shift — distance to real St. Landry prairie speakers (z-scored; lower=closer)

Both models clone the *same* Sophia reference, so speaker timbre cancels; the
difference in distance to the real prairie corpus = the learned accent prior.

| model | mean dist to real prairie |
|---|---|
| ep2 | **5.813** |
| prairie-ep1 | 6.005 |

**Honest negative: prairie-ep1 did NOT measurably move toward St. Landry.**
It is fractionally *farther* (Δ=−0.19), and the difference is **not significant**
(Mann-Whitney p=0.40). A few vowel-space features (F2, F3) *did* nudge toward
the real prairie speakers, but the overall shift is within noise.

**Caveat (metric sensitivity):** distance is ~5.8–6.0 in 19-dim space — large,
because it's dominated by *speaker* differences (Sophia vs the real prairie
voices), not accent. The accent signal is small relative to that, so this metric
may simply lack the sensitivity to detect a subtle prairie shift. n=18 per group.

## Verdict
- ✅ **Real result:** low-resource Cajun finetune ⇒ 0.60→0.20 WER on commodity hardware.
- ⚠️ **Lexicon:** improves *authenticity* (by design) at a small WER cost vs Parisian; needs human eval to credit.
- ❌ **Prairie retrain (1.03 h ×3):** no measurable accent gain. Honest negative — don't ship prairie-ep1 as "more Opelousas" on this evidence.

## Next steps to firm this into a paper
1. **Native-speaker A/B** (this is the metric that matters for accent + authenticity) — sheet built alongside this report.
2. **More prairie data / higher upweight** before re-testing the accent hypothesis (1 h ×3 was too little to move the needle).
3. **Speaker-controlled accent metric** — compare against a Sophia-voiced *standard*-French baseline so the distance isolates accent from speaker.
4. **Bigger/held-out WER set** + multiple takes per sentence for confidence intervals.
